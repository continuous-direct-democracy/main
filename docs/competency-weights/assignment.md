# Competency Assignment

## Who Is a Professional?

The central question of the weight system: **how to determine who is a professional** in a given field? A two-stage system is proposed: initial bootstrapping through formal education followed by peer-based determination.

## Stage 1: Bootstrap

Before the system launches and during the initial period, professionals are determined through **formal education**:

- Having a **diploma** from a college, institute, or university in the relevant specialty determines the initial competency level
- A diploma is a **mandatory requirement** for entering the competency system
- The level of education (bachelor's, master's, PhD, doctoral) may affect the initial competency level

**Example of initial distribution:**

| Education Level      | Initial Competency Points |
| -------------------- | ------------------------- |
| Bachelor's / College | 1                         |
| Master's             | 2                         |
| PhD (Candidate)      | 3                         |
| Doctoral             | 5                         |

## Stage 2: Peer-Based System

After the system launches, competency determination transitions to a **peer-based model** — professionals themselves determine each other's competency levels.

### Mechanism

Each year, a professional already included in the competency system receives the ability to **give 2 competency vote points** to any other person.

**Rules:**

1. **2 points per year** — each professional receives exactly 2 points to distribute
2. **Field restriction** — competency points can only be given to specialists **in one's own or adjacent fields**. A programmer can evaluate an IT architect but not a construction architect or energy systems architect. The list of adjacent fields is determined based on international specialty classifiers and can be adjusted through professional community voting
3. **Diploma required** — the recipient of points must have a diploma in the corresponding or adjacent field
4. **Full transparency** — everyone can see who gave their competency votes to whom
5. **Annual renewal** — points are allocated anew each year

### Examples

**Example 1:** Professor of genetics Ivanov believes that his graduate student Petrov has shown outstanding results. Ivanov gives both of his points to Petrov.

**Example 2:** Economist Sidorova sees that her colleague Kozlov is an excellent specialist in agricultural economics, and young researcher Volkova is a promising investigator. Sidorova gives 1 point to each.

**Example 3:** Biologist Smirnov doesn't believe anyone in his circle deserves additional points. He is **not obligated** to use his points — unused points are simply not distributed.

## Transparency

A key principle of the system is **full openness** of competency assignment:

- **Public information:**
  - Who gave competency points to whom
  - How many points each professional has
  - On what educational basis a person entered the system
  - History of competency changes over time

- **Why transparency is needed:**
  - Preventing collusion and "trading" of points
  - Enabling public oversight
  - Building professional reputations
  - Detecting anomalies and abuse

## Protection Against Abuse

### Limiting "Circular Boosting"

To prevent a group of people from exchanging points to artificially inflate competencies:

- Transparency makes such schemes **visible** to everyone
- Statistical analysis can detect **anomalous patterns** in point distribution
- The community can **challenge** a competency level through voting

### Education Requirement

The mandatory diploma requirement:

- Prevents entry into the system by people without basic professional knowledge
- Creates a **minimum threshold** of competence
- Is not the sole criterion — the peer-based system supplements formal education with real peer evaluation

## System Evolution

Over time, the competency system should **evolve**:

1. **Initial period** — formal education predominates (diplomas)
2. **Transition period** — coexistence of formal criteria and peer-based evaluation
3. **Mature system** — peer-based evaluation carries primary weight; diplomas remain as an entry threshold

It is expected that as the system develops, peer-based evaluation will become a more accurate indicator of real competencies than formal diplomas.
